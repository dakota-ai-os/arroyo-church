Y2K. The Mayan calendar in 2012. The uncertainty of 2020. And now, headlines about global conflict and artificial intelligence. If you've been alive for any of it, you've heard someone confidently announce that the end of the world was near. And yet here we still are.

So how should we actually think about the end of all things? There's really only one voice worth trusting on the subject, and that's the voice of Jesus. In this message we turned to one of His parables and asked a simple question: what did Jesus Himself say about the end of the world?

The main idea: what you believe about the end shapes how you live now

Here's why this matters even if "end times" talk makes you nervous: how you view the end of the world determines how you live in the present. Imagine you were promised a life-changing reward if you simply stayed to the very end of an event. You'd stay, wouldn't you? What we believe about how the story ends shapes how we spend our time, our energy, and our relationships today.

This parable comes right after Matthew 24, Jesus' longest teaching about His return. Matthew 25:1-13 distills that teaching into a single, unforgettable picture. Three truths rise to the surface.

1. God's delay makes a way

"At that time the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and went out to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish and five were wise... The bridegroom was a long time in coming, and they all became drowsy and fell asleep." (Matthew 25:1-5)

In the ancient world, a wedding didn't come with a printed "save the date." The groom could arrive at any hour, and the attendants had to be ready whenever he showed up. In the parable, the groom pictures Jesus, and the delay is real and intentional. There is a danger in the delay: the temptation to grow drowsy, careless, and spiritually lazy, telling ourselves that a Savior who hasn't returned yet must never be coming.

But the delay isn't a broken promise. Peter answered this very question for the early church:

"With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance." (2 Peter 3:8-9)

The delay reveals three things about God. First, He sees time differently; two thousand years to us is a blink to the eternal Author of history. Second, He is patient. If Christ had returned two hundred years ago, none of us would have had the chance to know Him at all. Third, His delay makes room for repentance. Repentance is a change of mind that grows, over time, into a changed life. God's patience isn't permission to keep living for yourself; it's an invitation to turn to Him.

2. You have to stay ready

"At midnight the cry rang out: 'Here's the bridegroom! Come out to meet him!'... The virgins who were ready went in with him to the wedding banquet. And the door was shut." (Matthew 25:6-10)

All ten women knew the groom was coming. The difference wasn't knowledge; it was preparation. Five brought extra oil and stayed ready. Five didn't. When you know Christ could return at any moment, three practices keep you ready:

  • Live like He could return today. What habits would you drop instantly if you knew Jesus was arriving in an hour? John wrote so that believers might "be confident and unashamed before him at his coming" (1 John 2:28). That's a healthy question to sit with.
  • Help others get ready. If heaven really is the joy the Bible describes, the most selfish thing a Christian can do is get ready alone. Love overflows; it invites people to the party.
  • Remember the reward. Readiness isn't grim duty. It leads to restoration.
"He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away." (Revelation 21:4)

3. The door is open now, but it will not stay open forever

"Later the others also came. 'Lord, Lord,' they said, 'open the door for us!' But he replied, 'Truly I tell you, I don't know you.' Therefore keep watch, because you do not know the day or the hour." (Matthew 25:11-13)

This is the sobering part. The door never closes on someone who never had a chance; it closes on those who had every chance and never walked through. And notice the reason those on the outside were turned away. It wasn't that they were too sinful. It was that Jesus said, "I don't know you." The way in isn't perfection; it's connection. Jesus said it plainly:

"I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved... The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy; I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full." (John 10:9-10)

Anyone can come. It doesn't matter who you are, what you've done, or what's been done to you. All you do is walk through by faith: trust that Jesus lived the life you couldn't, died in your place, and rose again. The wedding of heaven has already been paid for by His blood. You don't earn your way in. You get ready, and you show up.

So don't play games with God, assuming there's always more time. Jesus didn't call us to obsess over dates and calculations. He simply said: keep watch. Be faithful. Be ready.

A takeaway to carry with you

Here's the reflection question from this passage: If Jesus returned today, would He find you ready? If you're ready, stay ready. If you're not, the door is open right now, while your heart is still beating. Today is a good day to turn to Him.
